# Chapter 8: The Storm and the Ships

The storm came without warning.

One moment the sunset was bleeding across a calm horizon. The next, the wind howled down from the north like a living thing, slamming against the lighthouse tower with enough force to shake the iron stairs. Rain came in sheets, horizontal and stinging, driven by a gale that seemed to materialize out of nothing.

Sam gripped the lantern-room railing as the tower groaned. Below, the sea had turned white-capped and furious in minutes. Waves exploded against the cliff, sending spray high enough to mist the lantern glass.

And then lightning split the sky, and Sam saw them.

On the horizon, barely visible through the wall of rain, lights. Not a fishing boat. Not a coaster. A large vessel, low and industrial, bristling with antennae and equipment. Even at this distance, its silhouette was unmistakable—the survey lights, the crane arm, the A-frame at the stern for deploying submersibles.

Oceanus Deep Mining.

They were closer than Meridia had feared. Closer than Sam ever expected.

A low hum rose from beneath the tower, vibrating through the stone. Sam felt it in the soles of the feet, in the teeth. The Tide Callers. Their song had changed—the harmonies urgent, layered, almost frantic. They were afraid.

The beacon had to burn.

Sam turned from the window and dove for the controls.

---

The fuel tank was dry. The starter mechanism was crusted with rust. Sam cranked the ignition lever, but it moved maybe half an inch before seizing. The metal screeched—a sound of grinding corrosion, of forty years of salt and neglect fusing the parts into a solid block.

"No—no, come on—"

Sam wrenched at it again. Nothing. The storm hammered the tower. The humming from below rose in pitch, desperate. Through the window, the survey ship's searchlight swept across the sea, growing closer.

Sam slammed a fist against the brass housing. Stupid. Idiotic. Three years of disuse and you thought it would just work?

And then a memory surfaced.

A dusty room. A rainy afternoon. Sam, maybe eight years old, sitting at Aunt Eleanor's kitchen table with a broken music box—a little wooden thing that played a lullaby the grandmother had brought from the old country. The gears had jammed. Sam had been close to tears, tiny screwdriver useless in clumsy hands.

Eleanor had knelt beside the chair, her rough calloused fingers covering Sam's. "Sometimes the most important things just need patience and the right touch," she had said. "You don't force a stubborn gear. You listen to it. You find where it wants to move, and you help it."

She had guided Sam's hand to a single gear, turned it gently, and the whole mechanism had clicked back into alignment. The music box had played.

Patience. And the right touch.

Sam took a breath. Forced the hands to stop shaking. The diary. Eleanor's maintenance notes. The entry from April: *"Cleaned the lantern mechanism today. The old brass needed polishing—three hours' work, but she throws a fine beam now."* There were details. Diagrams, even. Sam had skimmed them, but the visual was there, burned into the memory.

The access panel. The main gear train. The fuel feed. The crystal recess.

Sam's hand went to the pocket. The small blue crystal Meridia had given warmth against the palm. Sam pulled it out and looked at the mechanism again. There, in the center of the fuel assembly—a slot. Exactly the size of the crystal. Not a decoration. A key.

Sam slid the crystal into place.

The lens hummed. A deep, resonant thrum that traveled through the brass, through the floor, through Sam's own bones. The prisms caught light from somewhere—not the fuel, not the lamp, but the crystal itself, glowing from within.

The survey ship's searchlight swept across the cliff face, fifty yards from the cave entrance.

Sam grabbed the ignition lever. This time, it moved. Smoothly. The brass clicked into alignment, gear engaging gear, and the fuel line opened. A flame caught in the lamp housing—small at first, then growing. The crystal flared blue-white, and the light flooded through the Fresnel lens, amplified, transformed.

Three long. Two short. One long.

The beam cut through the storm like a blade.

---

The survey ship's searchlight went dark.

The humming from below softened—relief, not fear. Sam watched through the rain-streaked glass as the corporate vessel drifted, its lights flickering, its instruments confused. The ship turned, slowly, and began to move away. Back toward open water. Back toward the world that had no business here.

The beacon burned steady. The storm raged around it, but the light did not waver.

Sam leaned against the brass housing, heart pounding, breath ragged. The glow of the modified lens painted the room in shifting blue and gold. The crystal pulsed gently in its slot, satisfied.

Footsteps on the iron stairs.

Sam turned. A figure emerged from the stairwell, silhouetted against the new light. Moving differently than Meridia. Human. Weary. Familiar.

A woman stepped into the lantern room. Her hair was white, her face lined by years of salt wind and something stranger. She wore clothes woven from seaweed and bioluminescent thread, and her eyes had the same deep darkness as the Tide Callers.

But her smile was the one from the photograph.

"I knew you'd figure it out," said Aunt Eleanor.
