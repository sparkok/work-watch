# Chapter 9: Reunion

Sam turned.

She stood in the doorway of the lantern room, one hand resting on the iron frame, the light of the beacon casting long shadows across her face. Her hair was silver now, not the brown of the photograph, and her skin was weathered deep—etched by salt and wind and something older than either. She wore the woven garments of the Tide Callers, and a strand of sea glass hung around her neck, each piece worn smooth by centuries of tide.

But her eyes were the same. Bright. Kind. Unmistakable.

Aunt Eleanor.

Neither of them moved for a long moment. The storm had begun to fade, its fury spent, and the only sound was the steady hum of the beacon and the distant mutter of retreating thunder.

Then Eleanor crossed the room. She didn't speak. She simply reached up and touched Sam's face, palm against the cheek, as though confirming that the image before her was real and not some trick of the deep-sea light. Her hands were rough—calloused in a way they had never been in Sam's childhood. Not keeper's hands anymore. Something else. Stone and salt and endless dark water.

"You're taller," Eleanor said. Her voice was hoarse, as though she had not spoken above a whisper in a long time. "I always wondered if you'd grow into those long legs."

Sam laughed—a broken, unexpected sound that turned into something close to a sob. "You're alive."

"I'm alive." Eleanor's hand dropped to Sam's shoulder. "I never wanted you to worry. I thought if I left quietly, the lawyer would sell the lighthouse, and you'd get the money, and that would be that. You'd go on with your life."

"Instead I came here."

"Instead you came here." Eleanor smiled, and the years fell away from her face. "I'm glad you didn't sell."

---

They sat on the lantern gallery, backs against the brass housing, the beacon turning above them in its slow, steady rotation. The storm had rolled out to sea, leaving behind a sky scrubbed clean and a horizon line sharp as a blade. Below, the sea still heaved, but the anger was gone from it.

Eleanor spoke of the world beneath. The cities in the dark. The crystal fields that pulsed with ancient light. The songs that carried through the water for miles. She spoke of it the way someone speaks of a home they have come to love.

"The survey ships will come back," she said finally. "The beacon buys us time, but not forever. Oceanus has too much invested. They'll send more ships, better equipment. Eventually, they'll see through the field."

"Then what?"

Eleanor was quiet for a moment. "The Tide Callers have decided. They cannot hide forever. They want to reveal themselves—on their own terms, before they're discovered. They want to meet the surface world as equals, not as secrets."

"That's huge," Sam said.

"It's terrifying." Eleanor smiled. "And necessary. But they need help. Someone who knows both worlds. Someone who can speak for them."

"Like you."

"Like both of us." Eleanor turned to face Sam fully. The first light of dawn was touching the horizon, painting the clouds in shades of rose and gold. "This lighthouse needs a keeper. At least for a while. I know you have a life up there—a job, a future, a world that makes sense. But I'm asking you to stay. Not forever. Just for the transition."

Sam looked out at the sea. The water was calm now. Somewhere beneath it, a whole civilization waited.

"How long is 'a while'?"

Eleanor shrugged, the gesture so human, so familiar, that Sam's heart ached. "A month. A year. Long enough to learn the light. Long enough to know the song."

The sun broke the horizon. The beacon's glow softened into the growing daylight, but it did not go out.

Sam's hand went to the pocket. The small crystal was warm against the chest, pulsing faintly.

"Okay," Sam said.

Eleanor said nothing. She just reached over and took Sam's hand, the way she had done a hundred times before, on a hundred rainy afternoons in a childhood that felt like another life.

They watched the sunrise together, two generations of lighthouse keepers, the beacon still glowing above them.
