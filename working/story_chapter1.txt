# Chapter 1: The Letter

The rain came down in sheets that Tuesday afternoon, washing the grime off the city streets in grey rivulets that swirled toward the drains. Sam sat at the desk in the corner of their modest apartment, the steady drumming against the window a familiar companion to the click of keyboard keys and the hum of the aging refrigerator. Another ordinary day. Another stack of emails. Another evening that promised nothing more than reheated pasta and the quiet glow of a television screen.

The mail slot clattered. The sound was so routine, so unremarkable, that Sam nearly ignored it. But something — a shift in the air, perhaps, or the faint creak of the floorboards in the hallway — compelled them to rise. The rest of the mail was the usual assortment of bills and flyers, slick with moisture and smelling of wet cardboard. But beneath them lay an envelope that did not belong.

It was cream-colored, heavy-stock paper, the kind that felt substantial in the hand. Sam's name and address were written in elegant black ink, the handwriting precise and unhurried. There was no return address, but the postmark read: *Greyhaven — 3 days prior*.

Greyhaven. The name stirred something deep and half-forgotten, like a stone dropped into still water. Sam carried the envelope back to the desk, turning it over in their hands before sliding a finger beneath the seal.

Inside was a letter on letterhead bearing the name *Holloway & Thorne, Solicitors*. Sam read it once quickly, then again slowly, the words settling like sediment in a glass.

*Dear Sam,* it began. *It is with regret that we inform you of the passing of your great-aunt, Eleanor Marchetti, who departed this life on the 14th of October.*

Sam stopped breathing for a moment. Great-aunt? They had no memory of any great-aunt. Their family was small, scattered, the branches mostly broken or lost to time. And yet —

A flash came. A woman with silver-streaked hair pulled into a loose bun. The smell of salt air and something sweet baking in an unfamiliar kitchen. Small, intricate seashells placed into Sam's cupped hands, each one a tiny spiral of ivory and coral. A voice, weathered and warm, saying: *These are from the shore near my lighthouse. Someday you'll see it for yourself.*

The memory faded as quickly as it had arrived, leaving behind only the faint ache of something precious and forgotten.

Sam returned to the letter. The solicitor's words were measured, clinical: Eleanor Marchetti had bequeathed to Sam the entirety of her estate, including a residential property described as "the Greyhaven Lighthouse, situated on the northern headland of Greyhaven Bay." The lighthouse had been in the family for three generations. It had stood vacant for the past three years, since Eleanor's health had failed.

*Please contact our office at your earliest convenience to arrange collection of the keys and the pertinent documents.*

Sam set the letter down and looked out the rain-streaked window. The city beyond was a blur of grey concrete and neon, indifferent and unyielding. Somewhere beyond that greyness, beyond the reach of the highways and the radio towers, there was a lighthouse. Abandoned. Waiting.

It was absurd. Sam had a job, a lease, a life that fit neatly into the grooves of routine. People did not simply abandon everything to chase an inheritance from a relative they could barely remember.

And yet.

The image of those seashells returned — the way they had caught the light in a kitchen that smelled of the sea. The way that woman had smiled.

Sam picked up the phone and dialed the number at the bottom of the letter.

"Hello," they said, their voice steadier than they felt. "My name is Sam. I'm calling about Eleanor Marchetti's lighthouse."

Outside, the rain began to ease, and through a crack in the clouds, a pale sliver of sunlight touched the wet pavement, pointing like a finger toward the unseen coast.
