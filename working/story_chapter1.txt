# Chapter 1: The Letter

The morning had begun like any other.

Sam had woken to the grey hum of the city—car horns bleating three blocks over, the upstairs neighbour's dog scratching at the floorboards, the smell of stale coffee from the café downstairs. Another Tuesday. Another shift at the archive office, sorting documents no one would ever read, stamping dates onto paper that would yellow and crumble long before anyone cared to open the folder again.

The letter was waiting on the doormat when Sam got home.

It wasn't the usual stack of bills and flyers. It was a single envelope, thick and cream-coloured, the paper heavy and expensive in a way that felt anachronistic. No logo. No return address. Just Sam's name, written in ink so dark it seemed to drink the light.

Sam slit it open with a butter knife, pulling out the contents with the vague suspicion that it was either an eviction notice or a wedding invitation. Instead, the first words caught in the throat like dust:

*Dear Sam,*

*If you are reading this, I have passed.*

*My name is Eleanor Rathbone. I am—was—your great-aunt on your mother's side. And I am leaving you the Greyhaven Lighthouse.*

Sam read the sentence three times. *Leaving you the Greyhaven Lighthouse.* It did not make sense. They had no memory of a Greyhaven. No memory of a lighthouse at all, unless you counted that postcard from a class trip to Cornwall, years ago, pinned to the corkboard above their desk.

But Aunt Eleanor...

Something stirred. A flicker, deep in the dark water of memory.

Sam was seven, maybe eight. It had been raining. A woman with silver-streaked hair and salt-cracked hands had visited the flat, bringing the smell of the sea into the small living room. She had crouched down to Sam's level and pressed something cold and ridged into their palm—a seashell, pale pink on the inside, still carrying the ghost of a distant shore.

*"This is a cowrie,"* she had said, her voice rough as harbour rope. *"They say if you hold one to your ear, you can hear the whole ocean."*

Sam had kept that shell for years, until it got lost in one of the moves. They had forgotten the woman's face, forgotten her name. But now it came back—the way her eyes had crinkled when she smiled, the way her hands smelled of brine and wind.

Aunt Eleanor.

Sam sat down heavily on the edge of the sofa, scanning the rest of the letter. The handwriting grew shakier toward the bottom, as if the effort of telling the truth had cost something.

*The lighthouse has stood empty for three years. I could not keep her running in the end—the eyesight went, then the knees, then the will. But I never stopped believing she deserved a keeper who would see her as I did: not just a tower of brick and glass, but a voice in the dark, calling ships home.*

*I do not know if you will come. Perhaps you have a life in the city that you love. Perhaps this letter will strike you as the ramblings of a lonely old woman, and you will throw it in the bin and forget me again.*

*But if there is even a grain of salt in your blood, Sam... come and see her. Just once.*

*The key is at the bottom of this envelope. The solicitor in Greyhaven has the rest.*

Sam turned the envelope over. A single brass key fell into their palm, heavy and cold, worn smooth by decades of turning in a lock.

Outside, the city noise pressed against the windows—sirens, traffic, the endless clatter of a thousand ordinary lives. But for the first time in years, Sam heard something else beneath it.

The murmur of a distant sea.

They stood up, crossed to the window, and looked out at the grey rooftops of the city. Somewhere beyond them—beyond the motorways and the train lines and the long, winding coast road—a lighthouse was waiting.

Sam picked up the phone and searched for trains to Greyhaven.

There was one at dawn.
