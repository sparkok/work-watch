# The Lighthouse Keeper's Secret

## Chapter 1: The Letter

The envelope was the color of old bone.

Sam noticed it the moment they pulled the mail from the slot — a rectangle of ivory linen paper standing out against the usual glut of bills and supermarket fliers. No return address, but the postmark read Greyhaven, and the name on the front was written in ink so dark it was almost black, looping and formal in a way that belonged to another century.

Sam carried it upstairs to their cramped one-bedroom apartment, set it on the kitchen counter, and stared at it while the kettle boiled. Greyhaven. The word stirred something deep and half-buried, like sediment rising from the bottom of a still pond. A coastal town. A place with salt-scoured cliffs and fog that rolled in off the sea like a slow gray tide.

They didn't recognize the name of the sender. A law firm: Strickland & Vane, Solicitors.

With the tea cooling untouched, Sam slid a thumb under the flap and pulled out a single sheet of heavy paper.

*Dear Mr. or Ms. Sam Turner,*

*We are writing to inform you that you are the sole beneficiary of the estate of one Eleanor Marwick, late of Greyhaven, who passed away on the fifteenth of March of this year. The primary asset bequeathed to you is the Greyhaven Point Lighthouse, its attached cottage, and the surrounding 2.3 acres of coastal land.*

*The property has been unoccupied for the past three years. We urge you to make arrangements to inspect it at your earliest convenience.*

Sam read the letter three times, the words refusing to settle. Eleanor Marwick. The name conjured nothing at first — just a stranger in a distant town, someone whose lighthouse was now somehow theirs. And then, like the first raindrop before a downpour, a memory broke through.

A woman with silver-streaked hair, kneeling on the floor of Sam's childhood living room. She had smelled of salt and cigarette smoke, and she had opened a cloth bag on the carpet, spilling out seashells — cowries and whelks and a single sand dollar so delicate Sam had been afraid to touch it. She had laughed and pressed the sand dollar into Sam's small hand.

"You keep that one," she'd said. "It belongs to you now."

Sam had been four, maybe five. They had not seen Aunt Eleanor since.

Now she was dead, and she had left them a lighthouse.

Sam sat down heavily at the kitchen table. Outside, the city hummed through the double-glazed windows — traffic, sirens, the distant clatter of a construction site. None of it felt real. The ivory letter lay on the table between their hands, and beside it their phone buzzed with a work email they had no intention of answering.

A lighthouse. Abandoned for three years. On a coast Sam had never visited.

The sensible thing would be to call the lawyer, ask about selling the property, and pocket whatever the land was worth. That was what a rational person would do.

But Sam looked at the letter again — at the looping ink, the word *lighthouse*, the suggestion of something waiting in a place they had forgotten — and felt a pull that had nothing to do with reason. It was the same pull they had felt as a child, holding that sand dollar, sensing that some things arrive in your life for reasons you cannot yet explain.

They opened their laptop and searched for trains to Greyhaven.

There was one leaving at dawn.

Sam booked a ticket, closed the laptop, and did not sleep that night. The lighthouse waited, gray and silent, in the part of their mind where dreams begin.
