# The Lighthouse Keeper's Secret

## Chapter 1: The Letter

Sam's life had settled into a comfortable rhythm of small, predictable disappointments. The alarm at seven. The coffee that was never quite hot enough. The commute through rain-washed streets where every face looked as tired as their own. At twenty-five, Sam had mastered the art of moving through the city without really being part of it — a ghost in a crowd of strangers, pressing the same buttons, walking the same pavements, breathing the same recycled air.

It was a Tuesday when the letter arrived.

Sam found it wedged between a takeaway menu and a final notice for a bill already paid — tucked into the narrow gap beneath the flat's front door as though it were ashamed to be seen with the rest of the post. The envelope was thick and cream-coloured, nothing like the cheap white rectangles that usually brought reminders of things left undone. There was no stamp, no franking mark. Just an address, written in careful cursive, and a return name embossed in the top-left corner: *Barrow & Finch, Solicitors, Greyhaven.*

Greyhaven. The name stirred something deep and dusty in Sam's mind, like a stone dropped into a well.

Inside was a single sheet of paper, heavy and watermarked, folded precisely into thirds. Sam read it standing in the narrow hallway, raincoat still dripping onto the floorboards.

*Dear Sam,*

*We write to inform you of the passing of your great-aunt, Eleanor Marsh, formerly of Greyhaven Lighthouse, Greyhaven. It is our duty and privilege to notify you that you have been named the sole beneficiary of her estate, which includes the aforementioned lighthouse and the land upon which it stands.*

Sam stopped reading and let the words sink in. Aunt Eleanor. The name surfaced not through family stories or old photograph albums, but through a single, fractured memory: a woman with silver-streaked hair and a laugh like wind chimes, sitting on the edge of Sam's childhood bed. She had brought a gift — a small glass jar filled with seashells, each one ridged and whorled and impossibly delicate. Sam remembered holding one up to the light, marvelling at how the thin curves let the afternoon glow through like stained glass.

*"Every shell has a story,"* Aunt Eleanor had said. *"You just have to listen."*

That was the only time Sam remembered meeting her. One visit, decades ago, and now she was gone.

The letter continued: *The lighthouse has been unoccupied for three years. We understand the property has weathered some damage from coastal storms, but the structure remains sound. As the sole heir, you are welcome to claim the property at your convenience, or alternatively, to direct us in arranging its sale.*

Sam folded the letter and slid it back into the envelope. Outside, the rain had softened to a grey drizzle, and the city hummed its endless, indifferent tune. The flat suddenly felt smaller — too many walls, too much noise from the street below. A lighthouse. An empty building on an empty coast, abandoned for three years, waiting for someone to remember it existed.

The decision came not as a dramatic revelation, but as a quiet certainty, like a lock finally clicking into place.

Sam reached for the laptop, opened a browser, and began searching for trains to Greyhaven.
