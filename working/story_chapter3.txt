# Chapter 3: The First Night

The oak door groaned shut behind Sam, sealing them inside a cylinder of cold stone and shadow. The air smelled of salt, rust, and something older—dust that had settled over decades of solitude.

Sam's footsteps echoed off the curved walls as they began to explore. The ground floor held a small kitchen with an iron stove, a wooden table, and a clock stopped at 11:47. A kettle sat on the counter as if waiting for someone who never returned. Everything wore a grey blanket of dust. White sheets draped the furniture like ghosts.

The spiral staircase wound upward, each step a protest of creaking iron. Sam climbed past the first landing—a small storage room cluttered with ropes, old charts yellowed at the edges, and tins of paraffin with labels faded beyond reading. The keeper's quarters occupied the next floor: a modest space with a single cot, its sheets still tucked tight at the corners. Someone had made that bed with care and never unmade it. Sam ran a finger along the pillowcase—it came away clean. Strange.

On the third floor, the library. Bookshelves sagged under the weight of worn novels, logbooks, and a leather-bound volume with no title on its spine. Sam pulled it open: blank pages, except for one near the middle, which held a single sentence in delicate handwriting: *The sea remembers what the land forgets.*

A shiver traced Sam's spine.

Up they climbed, higher still, until the tower narrowed and the staircase ended at a heavy iron door. Beyond it lay the lantern room. Sam pushed the door open.

The Fresnel lens dominated the space—a beehive of glass and brass, twelve feet tall, dense as a honeycomb, beautiful as a frozen chandelier. But it was dark. Dead. The great prisms caught no light, reflected only the grey evening seeping through the surrounding windows. The machinery that once turned the lens sat silent, cogs locked in place by rust and neglect.

Sam stood there a long moment, listening to the wind hiss around the glass panes, watching the sea churn far below. The horizon had swallowed the sun; dusk was bleeding into night.

*Stay*, a voice whispered inside them. Not a sound—an instinct. Sam decided. They would spend the night.

Down in the keeper's quarters, Sam lit a lantern they'd found in the kitchen, its flame small and brave against the encroaching dark. The cot was firm but tolerable. Sam lay down fully clothed, the lantern hissing softly on the bedside table. Sleep did not come. The lighthouse breathed around them—creaks and groans of settling stone, the mournful moan of wind through gaps in the mortar.

Then—a flicker.

Sam's eyes snapped open. The ceiling above pulsed with a pale blue-green light, soft as phosphorescence, dancing across the plaster like light through water. It was coming from the lantern room.

Sam bolted upright, heart hammering. The staircase groaned under their feet as they climbed, two steps at a time. They burst through the iron door—

Nothing.

The Fresnel lens sat dark and silent, exactly as they had left it. No flame, no movement, no reflection. The blue-green glow was gone.

But Sam's breath caught. The light had come from outside. Through the windows, out across the black expanse of the sea, something was glowing. Faint. Ethereal. A luminescence beneath the waves, far below the cliff where the water churned against the rocks. It pulsed—once, twice, three times—a slow heartbeat of cold fire deep in the ocean's throat. Then it vanished, as if a great eye had closed.

Sam stood frozen at the window, staring into the darkness where the light had been. The sea offered no answers. Only the crash of waves, the whistle of wind, and the terrible silence of the lighthouse around them.

Sam did not return to bed. They sat on the floor of the lantern room, back against the cold iron door, and watched the sea until the first pale grey of dawn crept across the water. Sleep was impossible. The question burned too brightly:

*What was that light?*
