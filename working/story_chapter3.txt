# The First Night

The door groaned shut behind Sam, swallowing the last of the afternoon light. For a long moment, they stood in the musty darkness, breathing air that tasted of salt and rust and something older—something undisturbed.

The ground floor was a single round room with a cold stone floor. A wooden chair lay on its side, one leg snapped. Cobwebs draped the corners like ghostly curtains. In the center, the spiral staircase rose into shadow, its iron steps worn smooth by decades of footsteps.

Sam touched the banister. It was cold enough to sting.

Up they climbed, the stairs groaning under each step. The first landing opened into the keeper's living quarters—a small kitchenette with a cast-iron stove, a shelf of dusty canned goods, a table with a cracked oil lamp. White sheets covered the furniture like a row of patient ghosts.

Beyond the kitchen, a narrow doorway led to a bedroom. Sam stopped in the doorway.

The bed was made.

The white sheets had been pulled tight, the corners tucked with military precision. A faded quilt lay folded at the foot. A single pillow sat centered at the headboard, slightly indented, as if someone had only just risen.

*Who made the bed?*

Sam's throat tightened. The lawyer had said no one had been inside for three years. But the bed wasn't dusty. The pillow wasn't flat. It looked *ready*.

They told themselves the previous keeper must have made it before leaving. It was a reasonable thought. It didn't feel like one.

Sam decided to stay the night. The decision came suddenly, stubbornly—a refusal to be spooked by a made bed and an old lighthouse. They gathered what little they had from the car, spread a blanket over the dusty sofa, and waited for the dark.

---

At midnight, the light woke them.

Sam hadn't realized they'd drifted off. But their eyes snapped open to a flickering blue-green glow that bled down the spiral stairs, pulsing gently against the walls of the living quarters. It wasn't harsh—it was soft, aquatic, like sunlight through deep water.

*The lens. It's lit.*

Sam's heart hammered. They scrambled up the stairs, the cold iron biting through their socks, taking the steps two at a time, bursting into the lantern room—

Nothing.

The Fresnel lens sat dark and silent in the center of the room, a massive iron-and-glass sculpture of concentric rings and prisms, beautiful and dead. No light. No warmth. No explanation.

Sam turned in a slow circle. The glass windows of the lantern room showed only the black sea and the darker sky. The glow was gone.

Then they saw it.

Far below the cliff, beneath the surface of the water, a light was moving. A pale blue-green luminescence, deep beneath the waves, drifting like a submerged star. It was too large to be a fish, too steady to be bioluminescence. It moved with purpose.

Sam pressed their forehead against the cold glass, breath fogging the pane.

The light pulsed. Once. Twice. Three times.

And then it was gone, as if someone had simply turned it off. The sea returned to black, to nothing, as if the light had never existed at all.

Sam stood at the window for a long time, waiting for it to return. The lighthouse groaned around them. The wind whispered through the iron grates. But the sea remained dark and still.

They didn't sleep.

When dawn finally broke—a thin gray line on the horizon—Sam was still at the window, eyes fixed on the water, watching for a light they weren't sure they wanted to see again.
