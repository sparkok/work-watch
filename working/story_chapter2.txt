# Chapter 2: Arrival at the Coast

The bus shuddered to a halt in Greyhaven's main square, and Sam stepped out into air that tasted like rust and salt. Fog draped the town like a wet sheet, muffling every sound except the distant, rhythmic crash of waves against stone. The buildings were old—weathered wooden facades painted in faded blues and greys, their windows looking out at the world with the tired patience of things that had seen too many winters. A row of seagulls sat motionless on a rooftop, watching Sam with unblinking yellow eyes.

The town was quiet. Not the comfortable quiet of a sleepy afternoon, but the kind that felt deliberate—as though the silence had been agreed upon long ago. An old woman sweeping her porch stopped when she saw Sam, her broom hovering mid-stroke. A fisherman mending nets by the dock glanced up, held their gaze for a beat too long, then looked away. The name "Marchetti" wasn't spoken, but it hung in the air between them all the same.

Mr. Thorne's office was above a bakery, in a room that smelled of old paper and cinnamon. He was a thin man with thinning hair and restless fingers that could not stop fidgeting with a paperclip. When Sam introduced themselves, his smile came too fast and faded too quickly.

"Ah, yes—Sam Marchetti. Or rather, Sam Hollis? The letter said—" He cleared his throat. "Never mind. The important thing is the lighthouse. It's yours now."

He pushed a heavy iron key across the desk. It was old and cold, blackened by decades of salt air. The teeth were long and unfamiliar, like something from another century.

"I'll need you to sign here, and here." His pen trembled slightly. "The property has been vacant since Ms. Marchetti passed. Three years now."

"I know," Sam said. "The letter explained."

"Yes, well." Mr. Thorne's eyes flicked to the window, where the fog pressed against the glass like a face. "The light hasn't been lit in three years. Not since she—" He stopped. Swallowed. "Not since."

The way he said it made the temperature in the room seem to drop. Not since she died, Sam finished silently. But Mr. Thorne did not say those words. He simply handed over a manila envelope with deeds and documents, shook Sam's hand with damp fingers, and wished them good luck in a tone that sounded more like goodbye.

---

The road to the lighthouse wound along the cliff's edge, narrow and unpaved, lined with gorse that bent away from the sea like refugees fleeing a disaster. Sam drove slowly, the rented car groaning over potholes and loose gravel. The fog began to thin, torn apart by a wind that carried the deep, cold smell of open water.

And then they saw it.

The lighthouse rose from the cliff like a white bone, tall and solitary, its paint peeling in long strips that revealed the dark stone beneath. It stood at the edge of everything, where the land gave up and the sea began. Waves crashed against the rocks below, sending up plumes of white spray that misted the air and left salt crystals glittering on the windows like frozen tears. The lantern room at the top was dark, its glass panels grey with grime, as though the lighthouse had closed its eyes and refused to open them.

Sam parked the car and stepped out. The wind hit them full in the face—cold, wet, alive. Seagulls circled overhead, their cries sharp and urgent. The lighthouse door was heavy wood, banded with iron, set into a stone archway weathered by a hundred winters. The key felt impossibly cold in Sam's palm.

They inserted it into the lock. It turned with a groan that seemed to come from the building itself, as if the lighthouse had been holding its breath for three years and was only now beginning to exhale.

Sam pushed the door open. Darkness and the smell of old dust poured out.

They stepped inside.
