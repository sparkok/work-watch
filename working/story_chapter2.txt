# The Lighthouse Keeper's Secret

## Chapter 2: Arrival at the Coast

The train deposited Sam onto a platform slick with salt spray at half past nine. Greyhaven Station was little more than a wooden shelter with a rusted bell, its paint flaking like dead skin. Beyond the tracks, the town hunched against the sea — a cluster of weathered buildings with shingled roofs and windows that stared out like squinting eyes.

Fog lay over everything, thick as wool. It swallowed sound and distance, turning the world into a damp hush broken only by the measured crash of waves somewhere invisible and the thin, lonely cry of gulls wheeling overhead. The air tasted of salt and iodine and something older — rotting kelp, wet stone, the breath of deep water.

Sam pulled their coat tighter and walked into the town. A bakery was open, its window lit yellow, but the man inside looked away the moment Sam glanced at him. An old woman on a bench, hands folded over a walking stick, watched Sam pass with an expression that was not quite suspicion and not quite fear — something in between, something guarded. No one said hello. No one held eye contact. The name Greyhaven hung in the conversations that stopped when Sam drew near, then resumed in murmurs too low to catch.

The solicitor's office stood at the end of High Street, a narrow two-story building squeezed between a boarded-up fishmonger and a pub called The Drowned Sailor. A brass plaque by the door read: *Thorne & Son, Solicitors — Est. 1892*. The son, Sam guessed, was either long gone or had never existed in the first place.

Mr. Thorne was waiting in the doorway. He was a thin man in his sixties with a nervous mouth and the kind of eyes that darted to corners. His suit was worn at the elbows, and he smelled faintly of pipe tobacco and mothballs.

"Mr. — ah — Sam," he said, extending a hand that trembled slightly. "Come in, come in. I have everything ready."

The office was cramped and dusty. Mr. Thorne did not offer tea or ask about the journey. He simply slid a manila envelope across the desk, along with a brass key the size of Sam's hand.

"The deed of transfer," he said, tapping the envelope. "And the key to the keeper's cottage. The lighthouse itself is locked separately — that key is on the ring as well."

"There's a cottage?" Sam asked.

"Attached. Small. It'll do." Mr. Thorne wiped his brow. "Now, I must advise you, the property has been unoccupied for three years. The light — " He paused, and his voice dropped half a register. "The light hasn't been lit in three years. Not since Eleanor."

He said it the way someone might say *the bridge is out* or *the well is poisoned*. A warning dressed as information.

"Why?" Sam asked.

Mr. Thorne stood abruptly. "The road is still passable, though the council won't maintain it. Take the coast road west for two miles. You'll see it." He was already moving toward the door, ushering Sam out with practiced politeness. "If you need anything, write to me. But I don't imagine you will."

And then Sam was standing on the pavement again, envelope in hand, the door clicking shut behind them.

The coast road was narrow and potholed, winding through gorse and bracken that grew dense and wild. The fog thinned as the land rose, and then the sea opened wide and grey, stretching to a horizon that seemed impossibly far. The waves came in long, patient swells, smashing against black rocks in plumes of white foam that hissed and withdrew, only to surge again.

And there it was.

The lighthouse stood at the edge of the world — a white tower rising from a rocky promontory, stark against the grey sky. It was taller than Sam had imagined, perhaps eighty feet, its paint faded and streaked with rust where the iron fittings held. A wooden gallery ringed the top, the railing broken on one side. The keeper's cottage, a low stone building, clung to its base like a child clutching a parent's leg.

The wind here was stronger, tasting of salt and cold and solitude. Sam parked the car and walked toward the lighthouse, boots crunching on crushed stone and limpets. The sea churned below, a constant, hungry sound. Gulls circled the tower's peak, their cries sharp and accusatory.

The door was heavy oak, studded with iron nails, warped by decades of salt wind. The brass lock was tarnished green. Sam slid the key in, felt the mechanism resist, and then turn with a grating click.

They pushed the door open.

A gust of stale, cold air rolled out — stone dust, dry rot, and something faintly metallic. Inside, it was dark.

Sam stepped over the threshold.
