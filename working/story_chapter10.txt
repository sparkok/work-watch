# Chapter 10: A New Light

Six months had changed everything.

The keeper's quarters no longer smelled of mildew and abandonment. The windows were clean, the floors swept, the old wood polished until it glowed amber in the candlelight. Books lined the shelves—paperbacks Sam had brought from the city, some of Eleanor's old volumes on tide charts and constellations. A photograph stood on the mantle: Sam and Eleanor on the day she returned to the surface, both grinning, the lighthouse rising behind them.

On the windowsill, propped against the glass, the small blue crystal caught the afternoon sun and scattered it into threads of light that moved across the walls like slow water.

Sam had learned the rhythm of the lighthouse. The fuel checks in the morning, the lens polish in the afternoon, the slow climb to the lantern room as dusk gathered. Each evening at twilight, Sam lit the beacon—three long, two short, one long—and watched the modified light sweep across the darkening sea. And each time, a pulse of warmth came back from the deep. A response. An acknowledgement.

The Tide Callers were singing back.

---

Eleanor had taken a small cottage in Greyhaven, the first house on the cliff road, with a garden that faced the sea. She visited the lighthouse twice a week, walking up the path with a basket of bread from the village bakery, sometimes with news, sometimes with nothing but a story.

She was different now. Her hair was fully white, and the silver lines the Tide Callers had woven into her skin had not faded—they traced her arms like veins of moonlight. She spoke more slowly than before, as though she had learned a different rhythm of time down there. But she laughed easily, and her eyes were the same.

"They've started talking," she said one evening, sitting at the kitchen table with a cup of tea. "The marine biologists came back for a second meeting. One of them is learning our signal language."

Contact was happening. Slowly. Carefully. The Tide Callers had sent a small delegation—Meridia and two others—to a private meeting with oceanographers and linguists. The news had not broken wide yet, but it was spreading through the right channels. A whisper of something ancient beneath the waves. A story the world was not quite ready to hear, but would soon have to.

As for Oceanus Deep Mining, their survey ships had redirected to other waters. A series of unexplained instrument failures, navigation glitches, and sonar blackouts had made the Greyhaven coast unattractive for further exploration. The corporation had written it off as a geological anomaly and moved on.

The lighthouse had kept its promise.

---

At sunset, Sam walked the cliff path.

The grass had greened with the turning season. Wildflowers dotted the slope—purple and yellow, bending in the salt wind. The sea below was calm, painted in shades of orange and violet by the dying sun. Sam stopped at the same outcrop where the journey had begun, looking down at the tide pools.

A ripple broke the surface.

A head emerged—small, pale, with wide dark eyes and hair that glistened like wet silk. A young Tide Caller child, no older than ten in human years, surfaced in the pool and blinked up at Sam.

For a moment, neither moved.

Then the child smiled, raised a hand in a shy wave, and slipped back beneath the water. A trail of bioluminescence marked the path, fading slowly.

Sam smiled and waved back.

---

Dusk had settled by the time Sam climbed the tower stairs. The lantern room was clean, the glass polished, the brass housing gleaming. The Fresnel lens stood at the center of the room like a great glass flower, its prisms waiting for light.

Sam crossed to the mechanism. The blue crystal was seated in the fuel assembly, glowing faintly even before the lamp was lit. Sam placed a hand on it, feeling the familiar warmth.

Six months ago, this lighthouse had been a cold iron shell full of unanswered questions. Now it was home.

Sam lit the beacon.

The flame caught, the crystal flared, and the light poured through the lens—blue-green, steady, sweeping across the sea in its ancient pattern. Three long. Two short. One long.

Far below, the water began to glow in response. Tiny points of light, scattered across the bay, rising like stars from the deep. The Tide Callers were answering. The song rose through the stone, the harmonies full and warm, and Sam felt it in the chest—a vibration that was not sound but connection.

The beacon no longer only warned. It welcomed.

Sam stood at the window, watching the blue-green light paint the waves, feeling the pulse of the answers returning from below. The wind stirred the hair. The sea breathed. The lighthouse hummed with life.

Somewhere in Greyhaven, Eleanor would be looking up at the light, knowing it was burning.

Somewhere beneath the waves, a child who had waved from a tide pool was telling a story about the strange land-dweller who smiled back.

And Sam, the keeper of Blackrock Lighthouse, stood between the two worlds—and belonged to both.

The beacon turned. The light swept on. The sea answered.

And everything was as it should be.
