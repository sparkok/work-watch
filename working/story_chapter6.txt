# Chapter 6: The Tide Callers

The tunnel swallowed Sam whole.

Water lapped at the edges of the narrow passage, the bioluminescent pool rising to mid-calf, then to the knees. The glow clung to Sam's skin like phosphorescent dust, each step sending ripples of light scattering across the surface. The walls pressed close, rough and warm, and the humming vibrated through them—through everything—until it was no longer a sound from outside but a rhythm inside Sam's own chest.

Then the tunnel opened, and the humming became voices.

Sam stopped at the threshold, flashlight dimmed to uselessness. The grotto before them was immense—a cathedral of living stone whose ceiling dissolved into darkness far above. But the light down here needed no sun. The water filled the entire chamber like a submerged meadow, glowing brilliant turquoise, bright as a tropical shallows. Veins of silver-green light traced through the rock walls, pulsing in slow, synchronized waves.

And in the water stood figures.

The song came from them. Dozens of voices, weaving together in harmonies that seemed to have no beginning and no end. They stood waist-deep in the glowing water, some younger, some ancient, their bodies pale and smooth, catching the turquoise light like moonlight on wet stone. Their eyes were large and dark—pupils so wide the iris was almost invisible—adapted to a world without sun. Their clothes shimmered: threads of woven seaweed intertwined with fibers that glowed faintly, tracing patterns across their bodies that mirrored the carvings on the cavern walls.

They were beautiful. They were wrong. They were singing.

And then they stopped.

The silence was absolute. The water still glowed, the walls still pulsed, but the voices cut off as though a door had slammed shut. Every pair of dark eyes turned toward Sam. No one moved.

Sam stood frozen at the tunnel's mouth, dripping phosphorescence, caught in the stillness.

A figure parted from the group.

She moved through the water without disturbing it—no ripples, no splash. Her skin was the color of old ivory, traced with faint silver lines that spiraled along her arms and neck. Her hair, white as sea foam, hung past her waist, interwoven with threads of living light. She was old—impossibly old—her face weathered by centuries of salt and time. But her eyes were sharp and clear, and they studied Sam without fear.

She stopped a few feet away. The water rose to her ribs, but she stood as comfortably as if on dry land.

"Child," she said. Her voice was low and melodic, carrying the quality of the song even in speech. The accent was unfamiliar—ancient, lilting, the vowels stretched in ways that felt prehistoric. "You have come down the stairs."

Sam found the voice. "I—yes. I found my aunt's diary. The door behind the clock. I had to see."

The elder tilted her head, a gesture that seemed more aquatic than human. "Eleanor. Yes. She spoke of you. There was a picture in her quarters—a young face by a river. She looked at it often."

Sam's throat tightened. "She kept a picture of me?"

"She kept all that mattered." The elder's gaze was warm, but distant, as though she measured centuries where Sam counted years. "I am called Llyr. In your tongue, it means—" She paused, searching. "It means the sound the tide makes when it turns. I have been the speaker for my people for longer than your village has stood."

"What are you?" The question came out before Sam could stop it.

A smile touched Llyr's lips—an expression that belonged to a different kind of face. "We are the Tide Callers. We have lived beneath this coast since before your ancestors crossed the land bridges. Before the ice retreated. Before the forests grew where the sea now stands." She gestured around the grotto. "This is our home. The caves run for hundreds of miles beneath the seabed. We have cities down there, in the dark. You would not understand them. But we also have need of the world above."

"The lighthouse," Sam said.

Llyr nodded. "Eleanor understood. The beacon is not only for ships. Its light carries patterns we can read—warnings, signals. When the weather brings danger, when the surface people send their divers too close, when..." She paused, and for the first time, something hard entered her voice. "When *the others* come closer."

"The others?"

"There are things in the deep that are not us. Old things. Hungry things. The light keeps them at bay." Llyr's dark eyes met Sam's. "Eleanor kept the lamp burning every night for thirty years. She was the keeper of the light and the keeper of our secret. When she chose to stay with us, the beacon went dark."

"Three years ago," Sam whispered.

"Three years," Llyr confirmed. "We have hidden. We have sung the old protection songs. But the deep grows bolder. We need the light again. We need someone who knows the machine, who can climb the tower, who can read the signals."

She stepped closer. The silver lines on her skin flared softly, responding to something—emotion, perhaps, or intent.

"Will you light the beacon again, child of Eleanor?"

The grotto was silent. The dark eyes of the Tide Callers watched from the glowing water. The turquoise light pulsed, slow and steady, like a heartbeat beneath the sea.

Sam looked back toward the tunnel that led to the stairs. To the lighthouse. To the world above.

And thought of the dark lantern room, cold and silent for three years.
