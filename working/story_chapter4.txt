# Chapter 4: The Hidden Diary

Sleep did not come easily, and when it did it was thin and restless, threaded with dreams of light pulsing through dark water. Sam woke with the sun already high, the brass lamp in the lantern room a dead thing now, grey and cold. The sea below was a flat sheet of blue, innocent and serene, as if it had never held secrets.

But Sam remembered.

The keeper's quarters were a single cramped space: a narrow iron bed, a wooden desk, a rickety chair, a single shelf of salt-warped books. The previous search had been cursory, done in the exhausted haze of a long journey. Now, in the clear morning light, Sam began again, systematically.

It was the bed that gave up the secret. When Sam knelt to check beneath the frame, a floorboard shifted — not by much, but enough. A nail had worked itself loose over years of neglect, and when Sam pried, the board came free. In the hollow beneath lay a leather-bound journal, its cover soft and cracked, the pages swollen with humidity. Sam lifted it carefully, as if handling something alive.

*Aunt Eleanor's diary.*

Sam sat cross-legged on the floor and opened it. The first entries were what one might expect: careful notations of date, wind speed, barometric pressure. Observations of the daily routine.

> *March 12. Replaced the frayed rope on the flagpole. Gulls are nesting on the west ledge again. Mrs. Hargrave's son brought fresh bread and cheese. Quiet night.*

> *April 3. Storm coming from the northeast. Latitude bearings logged. Lantern checked and cleaned. All well.*

Sam turned the pages, reading faster. The entries grew shorter as the months passed, the handwriting looser, less controlled.

> *June 18. Heard something tonight. Not wind. Not water. Thought it was the radio at first, but it was off. A low hum, almost like singing. Came from below, not above.*

The hair on Sam's arms stood. Below — not from the lantern room, but from beneath the lighthouse itself. From the sea.

> *July 2. It happened again. I went outside. The water was calm, but there were lights — faint, blue-green, pulsing in threes. I'm not the first keeper to see them. Old Davies mentioned it once, before he left. He said they'd been here longer than the lighthouse.*

Sam's breath caught. *Pulsing in threes. Blue-green.* Exactly what Sam had seen last night.

> *August 10. I hear them almost every night now. They sing, and I understand somehow, even though there are no words. They're asking something. I don't know what. But I feel it pulling.*

The diary's final third was jagged, the ink sometimes smudged — tears? seawater? — the writing barely legible in places.

> *September 5. Found the stairs below. Not the spiral up to the lantern — another set. Hidden. Behind a false wall in the storage cellar. They go down. Deep down. I followed them for a while. The air grew warm. The singing grew louder. I was afraid and came back.*

Sam's hand trembled, turning the page.

> *October 2. They've asked me to stay. I think I will. If anyone finds this — the door is behind the grandfather clock.*

The final entry was dated three years ago, almost to the day. There were no more pages.

Sam looked up.

Against the wall of the living quarters, half-hidden behind a stack of old newspapers, stood an antique grandfather clock. Its pendulum hung motionless. Its hands were frozen at midnight.

Sam rose slowly, the diary clutched to the chest. The clock was heavy, solid oak, but the casters beneath it groaned when Sam shoved. It slid aside with a low, grinding sound.

Behind it, set into the stone of the lighthouse wall, was a narrow iron door. Rust bloomed across its surface in orange growths. No handle, only a keyhole — ancient, shaped like a cross.

And a faint, rhythmic warmth breathed from the gap beneath it, as if the door itself was alive.
