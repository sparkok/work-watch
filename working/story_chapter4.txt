# Chapter 4: The Hidden Diary

Morning light poured through the salt-crusted windows of the keeper's quarters, painting pale rectangles across the wooden floor. Sam woke stiff-necked from a sleep full of strange dreams—dreams of voices underwater, of lights pulsing in the deep, of something vast and patient waiting below.

He'd checked the tide pools at dawn. Nothing unusual. No blue-green glow. Just ordinary rock and seaweed and startled crabs scuttling for cover.

But the feeling hadn't left him.

Sam ate a quick breakfast of tinned peaches and stale crackers, then turned his attention to the room itself. Uncle Gerald had said the cottage was cleared out, but "cleared out" and "thorough" were two different things. Sam started with the dresser drawers—empty, save for a few mothballs and a rusted sewing needle. He checked the wardrobe. Nothing. He ran his hands along the walls, pressed against the floorboards, searching for anything the official clearing might have missed.

It was the bed that gave up the secret.

When Sam dropped to his knees and peered underneath, the floor looked ordinary at first—wide pine planks, warped with age and salt air. But one board caught his attention. Its nails sat slightly higher than the others, their heads unworn, as though they'd been driven in recently and never stepped on. Sam slid a knife blade into the gap and pried.

The board came up with a groan.

Beneath it, wrapped in oilcloth, lay a book.

Sam lifted it carefully. The leather cover was cracked and spotted with mildew, but the brass clasp still held firm. He worked it open with trembling fingers and turned to the first page. In elegant, looping handwriting:

*Property of Eleanor Vance. Keeper's Log, Blackrock Lighthouse.*

---

*April 12th*
*Wind from the northeast, force five. Cleaned the lantern mechanism today. The old brass needed polishing—three hours' work, but she throws a fine beam now. Supply boat came at noon. Mrs. Higgins sent biscuits. Too many for one person, but I won't complain.*

*April 29th*
*Quiet week. Fog rolled in on Tuesday, didn't lift till Thursday noon. Heard the sea whistles from the shipping lane. One tanker, one fishing trawler. Usual.*

*May 8th*
*Gale warning. Battened everything down. The tower groaned all night—the old bones complaining. Slept in the service room at the base of the stairs. Felt safer there.*

The entries continued like that for months. Weather. Repairs. Supply orders. The lonely rhythm of lighthouse life. Sam flipped forward, scanning for anything unusual.

Then came June.

---

*June 3rd*
*Strange night. Couldn't sleep—too warm, too still. Went up to the lantern room around midnight. The sea was glass. Not a ripple. And then—I swear I heard something. A sound in the water, like singing, but not quite. Low and long. It went right through me. I stood there until it faded. Told myself it was the wind. But there was no wind.*

*June 17th*
*It happened again tonight. I didn't imagine it. A light beneath the surface—green-blue, pulsing like a heartbeat. And the song. I heard it clearer this time. It wasn't the wind. The old fishermen used to tell stories about what lives under Blackrock. I never believed them.*

*July 2nd*
*I've started going up every night. I tell myself it's duty. But it's not. I need to know if they'll come again. Tonight, the light answered back. I flashed the beacon once, twice—and the glow below pulsed in response. Three times. The same rhythm. I've never written this down before because I thought I was losing my mind. But I'm not. Something is down there, and it knows I'm here.*

*July 14th*
*The stairs below.*

*I found them today. A hidden door in the foundation—I'd always assumed the tower was solid stone from the cliff up. But there's a staircase. Spiral. Iron. Descending into the rock itself. I don't know where it leads. I'm not brave enough to find out. Not yet.*

*July 30th*
*I went down. Just ten steps. The air changed—warm, damp, smelling of salt and something else. Something metallic. I heard dripping. And far below, the song. Muffled by stone. Waiting.*

*I came back up. I sat in my chair until dawn, shaking.*

---

Sam's hands were cold. The entries grew shorter after that, more erratic.

*August 11th*
*They speak to me now. Not in words—in feelings. Images. A city of towers below the waves. Lights in every window. They want me to see it.*

*August 29th*
*I've been going down the stairs. Every night. I've stopped fighting it. The song is beautiful. It doesn't want to harm me—it wants to show me something. I've never felt so understood.*

*September 15th*
*Tonight I saw the door. At the bottom of the stairs. Iron, crusted with salt, older than the lighthouse. I touched it. It was warm.*

And then—the final entry. The ink was darker, the handwriting shakier.

*October 3rd*
*They've asked me to stay. I think I will.*

*If anyone finds this—the door is behind the grandfather clock.*

Sam looked up.

Across the room, pressed against the wall between the fireplace and the window, stood an old grandfather clock. The wood was dark, almost black with age. The brass pendulum hung motionless. The hands were frozen together at the very top of the face.

Midnight.

Sam rose slowly. The floorboards creaked beneath his feet. He crossed the room and set his hands on the clock's carved frame. The wood was cold. He pushed.

The clock slid aside with a long, low groan, revealing the wall behind it.

And there.

Set into the stone, barely visible beneath layers of rust and salt, was a door. Narrow. Iron. No handle on this side.

It was warm to the touch.
