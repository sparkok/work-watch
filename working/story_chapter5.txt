# Chapter 5: The Stairs Below

Sam pressed both palms against the cold iron and pushed.

The door did not swing open so much as surrender. Its hinges, seized by decades of salt and neglect, let out a long, groaning shriek that echoed through the lighthouse like a wounded animal. Rust flakes showered onto Sam's sleeves. Beyond the threshold, darkness so absolute it felt solid. And from that darkness came a rush of air — cold, damp, carrying the sharp mineral bite of the sea.

Sam clicked on the flashlight.

The beam revealed a spiral staircase descending into the earth, its steps hewn directly from the living rock of the cliff. Unlike the clean iron spiral that rose to the lantern room above, this was raw and ancient. The treads were uneven, smoothed by centuries of use. The walls glistened with a sheen of salt, crystalline in the flashlight's glare.

Sam stepped through the doorway, one hand tracing the slick wall for balance. The iron door groaned behind them, settling back against its jamb but not quite closing. A sliver of light from the study above remained, a golden thread tethering Sam to the world they knew.

Down. Around. Down again.

The staircase corkscrewed into the cliff, and with each revolution the sound of the sea grew louder. Not the distant crash of surf against rock, but something closer and more intimate — the gurgle of water moving through hidden channels, the drip of moisture from unseen ceilings, the deep percussive rhythm of waves against a submerged shore. The air grew heavier, thicker, heavy with the scent of brine and cold stone.

Sam counted the steps. Fifty. Eighty. One hundred and ten. The staircase had descended far past the lighthouse's foundation, far past the level of the surrounding land. The rational part of Sam's mind did the calculation: they were below sea level now, spiraling into the submerged heart of the cliff.

At one hundred and forty-seven steps, the staircase ended.

Sam stepped into a cavern so vast the flashlight could not find its ceiling. The floor was uneven rock, slick with moisture, sloping down toward a pool of water that filled the cavern's center. And the water — the water burned with light.

It was the same blue-green phosphorescence Sam had seen from the study window, but here it was overwhelming. The pool was a lake of liquid starlight, pulsing gently with a light that seemed almost alive. Bioluminescence, Sam thought, the same mechanism that made fireflies glow and jellyfish drift through the abyss in trails of cold fire. But this was different. The light moved with purpose, swirling into patterns that resolved and dissolved before the eye could fix upon them, as though something beneath the surface was dreaming in light.

Sam knelt at the water's edge. The glow responded, intensifying where Sam's shadow fell, as if it recognized the presence of another living thing. Tiny organisms, yes — but also something more. Something that watched.

And then Sam saw the walls.

Carved into the stone around the cavern, spiraling patterns covered every surface. Not writing, not quite. They flowed and curled like kelp in a current, like the lines of an equation written in a language that existed before speech. Sam turned the flashlight toward one section, and the lines seemed to shift — not carved deeper, but *moving*, just at the edge of vision, like the periphery of a dream.

A sound rose from the darkness beyond the pool.

Low. Melodic. A hum that vibrated in Sam's chest before reaching their ears. It was like a song sung underwater, filtered through stone and salt and the deep cold of the abyss. The bioluminescence pulsed in time with it, each swell of light following the melody's rise and fall.

Sam's flashlight found the far end of the cavern. A tunnel, rough-hewn and narrow, sloped downward into darkness. The humming came from within it — from somewhere far below, where the cliff gave way to the sea itself.

The song pulsed again, and Sam felt it in their bones.

*They've asked me to stay. I think I will.*

Sam's hand tightened on the flashlight. The cavern was not the end. It was only the beginning.
