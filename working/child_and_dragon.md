# The Child and the Dragon

---

## I. The Mountain That Breathed

On the far side of the Ash Hills, past the Black River and the twisted forest of ironwood, there stood a mountain that was not a mountain.

The villagers of Lower Thornwick had known this for three generations. Their grandparents had seen the great wings unfurl against the sunset. Their parents had felt the ground tremble when the beast turned in its sleep. They themselves had grown up with the rule carved into the village gate: *Do not climb the mountain. Do not look too long at the peak. Do not speak of what sleeps there.*

The dragon's name was Vethanar, and he had been there longer than the village, longer than the kingdom, longer than the stories that tried to contain him.

He was old. Older than the ironwood trees. Older than the Ash Hills themselves. His scales had faded from crimson to a deep, stone-gray, and his fire had banked to a low ember in his chest. He did not hoard gold or kidnap princesses or burn crops for sport. He simply *slept*, most of the time, curled around the molten heart of the mountain.

But a sleeping dragon is still a dragon. And a mountain that breathes is never truly still.

---

## II. The Girl Who Went Up

Her name was Elara, and she was eleven years old.

She was small for her age, with tangled black hair and a scab on her left knee that never seemed to fully heal. She lived with her grandmother at the edge of the village, in a cottage with a leaky roof and a garden that produced more weeds than vegetables.

Elara was not a remarkable child. She could not read well. She could not sing. She was afraid of the dark and of the butcher's dog and of speaking to strangers.

But she was stubborn. Stubborn in the quiet, patient way of water wearing down stone.

And so, when her grandmother fell ill in the deep of winter — when the healer's herbs did nothing and the fever climbed night after night — Elara decided to climb the mountain.

Because there was a story. An old, forgotten story that the grandmother had told her once, half-delirious with sickness. *Dragon's tears*, the old woman had murmured. *They say dragon's tears can heal anything. But no one has asked in a thousand years.*

Elara did not know if it was true. She did not care.

She packed a cloth bag with bread and a waterskin, kissed her grandmother's burning forehead, and walked out the door before dawn.

---

## III. The Ascent

The climb took three days.

The first day was easy — a winding path through the ironwood forest, where the trees grew so close their branches blotted out the sun. Elara walked in perpetual twilight, listening to the creak of ancient wood and the distant skitter of things she chose not to identify.

The second day was harder. The path vanished. The slope steepened, and the loose scree slid beneath her feet like grains of an hourglass. She cut her hands on sharp rocks. She drank half her water. She sat on a ledge and cried, alone, with no one to see.

She kept going.

On the third morning, she reached the summit.

There was no dragon.

There was a crater — vast and circular, easily the size of the entire village. At its center, a dark pool steamed in the cold air. The ground was warm beneath her feet. The air smelled of sulfur and ash and something else, something ancient, like old stone heated by a thousand summers.

She stood at the edge of the crater and felt very, very small.

"I've come to ask for help," she said. Her voice was barely a whisper, swallowed by the vast space.

Nothing happened.

She tried again, louder. "My grandmother is sick. She's dying. I was told — I was told dragon's tears can heal anything."

The wind howled across the crater. The pool rippled.

And then the mountain opened its eyes.

---

## IV. The First Words

The ground trembled. The steaming pool parted, and from its depths rose a head the size of a cart — gray-scaled, ancient, with eyes like pools of molten gold.

Elara did not run. She could not. Her legs had forgotten how.

The dragon regarded her in silence for a long, terrible moment. His breath was warm and smelled of deep earth. When he spoke, his voice was like stones grinding together at the bottom of the ocean — deep, slow, heavy with time.

"A child," he said. "A human child. On my mountain. For the first time in —" he paused, calculating — "three hundred and seventy-two years."

"I'm sorry for trespassing," Elara managed. "But my grandmother —"

"Is dying," the dragon finished. "Yes. All humans are, eventually. That is what you do."

"No," Elara said, and the word came out fiercer than she intended. "Not *eventually*. *Now*. She is dying *now*. And there is a story that your tears can heal."

The dragon's head drew closer. His eyes, each one larger than her entire body, studied her with an intensity that made her bones ache.

"Do you know," he said slowly, "what a dragon's tear *is*?"

Elara shook her head.

"It is not a potion. It is not a medicine. A dragon's tear is sorrow made solid — a grief crystallized into form. To give you one, I would need to remember something I have spent centuries trying to forget."

He pulled back, and his voice dropped. "Go home, child. Your grandmother will die. You will mourn. You will grow old, and you too will die. That is the way of your kind. It is not cruel. It is simply *true*."

---

## V. The Argument

Elara did not leave.

She sat down on the warm stone at the crater's edge, folded her arms, and stared at the dragon with the immovable stubbornness of a child who has decided that reality is negotiable.

"No," she said.

The dragon blinked. "No?"

"*No*. You don't get to say 'that's just the way things are' and go back to sleep. That's what adults do when they don't want to try. My grandmother didn't raise me to accept things without trying first. So I'm not leaving until you cry."

The dragon stared at her.

"You are —" he began, then stopped. He seemed to be searching for a word that had not been used in several centuries. "— *annoying*."

"Good," said Elara.

They sat in silence for a long time. The sun crawled across the sky. The wind died down. Elara ate the last of her bread and shared a piece with the dragon, who regarded it with deep suspicion before consuming it in a single, delicate bite.

"That was adequate," he admitted.

"I know," she said.

---

## VI. The Story the Dragon Told

Night fell. The stars came out, cold and brilliant. Elara shivered, and the dragon — without comment — curled his massive body around her, blocking the wind.

"A dragon's tear," Vethanar said at last, "is made of a single moment. The moment I watched my last hatchling suffocate in the ash of a volcanic eruption. The mountain breathed wrong that day. The tunnels filled. I was too large to reach her. I heard her calling until I could not hear her anymore."

His voice was flat, empty, like a wound that had scarred over but never healed.

"I have lived for two thousand years. I have seen mountains rise and crumble. I have watched kingdoms bloom and rot. But that one moment — that one failure — has never faded. If I let myself weep for her, even now, my tears would fill this crater. But they are not medicine. They are *grief*. They would not heal your grandmother. They would drown her."

Elara was quiet for a long time.

Then she said: "I think you're wrong."

The dragon's head lifted.

"I think grief and love are the same thing," she said slowly, working it out as she spoke. "I think you loved her so much that the grief never went away. And I think — I think if you had stopped loving her, that would be worse. Not the grief. The *stopping*."

She reached out and laid her small hand on the dragon's massive snout.

"I don't want you to stop loving her," she said. "But maybe — maybe you don't have to keep it all inside."

For a long, terrible moment, nothing happened.

Then the dragon's great shoulders began to shake.

He wept. Not gently, not gracefully. He wept like a mountain breaking apart — great, heaving sobs that shook the crater and sent rocks tumbling down the slope. His tears fell like warm rain, drenching the stone, pooling in Elara's outstretched hands.

She cupped them carefully. Three tears. Warm and golden, glowing faintly in the starlight.

She held them as he cried. She stayed beside him through the night, a small hand on a great gray snout, while a two-thousand-year-old dragon remembered how to mourn.

---

## VII. The Journey Home

Elara descended the mountain in a daze.

The tears were in a small clay jar, still warm, still glowing faintly. She moved on autopilot — down the scree slope, through the ironwood forest, across the Black River. She arrived at her grandmother's cottage at dawn on the fourth day.

The old woman was barely breathing. Her skin was gray. The healer had already left, certain there was nothing more to be done.

Elara uncorked the jar. A warm, golden light filled the room.

She touched one tear to her grandmother's lips.

The effect was immediate. Color flooded back into the old woman's cheeks. The fever broke. Her breathing eased, deepened, became the slow rhythm of peaceful sleep.

By evening, she was sitting up, asking for soup, scolding Elara for wandering off without a coat.

The healer called it a miracle. The villagers whispered about it for years.

Elara never told anyone what had really happened. Not the full story. Some things, she decided, belonged only to the ones who lived them.

---

## VIII. The Return

A year later, Elara climbed the mountain again.

The dragon was waiting for her at the summit. He looked different — lighter, somehow, as if a weight had been lifted from his ancient bones. His golden eyes held a warmth that had not been there before.

"I brought you bread," Elara said, holding up a loaf. "And also — I wanted to see if you were all right."

The dragon made a sound like grinding stones. It took Elara a moment to realize he was *chuckling*.

"I am better than I have been in a thousand years," he said. "I forgot — I had forgotten — that grief shared is grief halved. You taught me that."

"I'm eleven," Elara said. "I don't know anything."

"That is precisely why you know everything that matters," the dragon replied.

They sat together on the crater's edge, watching the sun set over the Ash Hills. The dragon did not weep that day. He simply *was* — present, warm, alive in a way he had not been for centuries.

Before she left, he pressed a single scale into her hand. It was warm, iridescent, shimmering with colors that did not exist in the ordinary world.

"If you ever need me," he said, "hold this and speak my name. I will come."

Elara nodded, tucked the scale into her pocket, and walked down the mountain.

She was twelve now. She was not afraid of the dark anymore. She was not afraid of much, anymore.

She had faced a dragon. She had held his grief in her hands. And in doing so, she had learned something that no book could teach and no story could fully contain:

*The hardest tears to cry are the ones that heal the most.*

---

## Epilogue

Elara grew up. She became a healer, the best in the valley. She never ran out of medicine — not because she had found a dragon's tears, but because she had learned, from a grief-stricken dragon, that the most powerful remedy in the world is simply *showing up* for someone in their pain.

The scale stayed with her always. She never used it. She never needed to.

But sometimes, late at night, when the wind howled and the mountain loomed dark against the stars, she would hold it to her ear.

And she would hear, faintly, like stones grinding together at the bottom of the ocean:

*I am here. You are not alone.*

And that, she knew, was the only miracle that had ever mattered.
