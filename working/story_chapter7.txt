# Chapter 7: Eleanor's Legacy

The climb back up the spiral staircase felt longer than the descent had been. Sam's legs burned, the flashlight beam dancing across the narrow walls, but the mind was not on the stairs. It was on the grotto. The voices. The dark eyes watching from the glowing water.

And on Meridia, who climbed behind without effort, her bare feet finding the worn stone steps as naturally as a fish finds current.

At the iron door, Sam stepped through into the keeper's quarters. The grandfather clock still stood pushed aside, the afternoon light falling across the dusty floor. It felt like another world now—pale and ordinary, stripped of the wonder that lay beneath.

Meridia followed into the room. She moved differently above the water, slower, as though the air was thicker for her. Her seaweed garments rustled dryly, and the bioluminescent threads in her hair had dimmed to a faint glimmer.

"The lantern room," she said. "Take me up."

They climbed the lighthouse tower together. The spiral staircase here was iron, not stone—narrow and open, the wind whistling through the gaps. Meridia paused at each landing, touching the walls, her dark eyes distant. She had not been up here in three years.

The lantern room was exactly as Sam had left it. The great Fresnel lens sat in its brass cradle, a jewel of prisms and glass, throwing fractured rainbows across the walls. The mechanisms were still, the fuel tank dry.

Meridia circled the lens, her fingers trailing across the glass. "You see only a lighthouse," she said. "But what your ancestors built, they built upon what we gave them."

"What do you mean?"

Meridia stopped at the base of the lens. She pressed her palm against a crystal panel that caught the light differently from the others—a faint blue tint, barely noticeable. "This lens was modified. The original glass came from your world. But the heart of it—" She tapped the blue crystal. "This came from the deep. Our people mined it, shaped it, gave it to the lighthouse builders three hundred years ago, when we first made the compact."

"The compact?"

"The agreement. They would build the lighthouse. We would keep them safe from the storms, guide their ships away from the rocks. And in return, the beacon would always burn in our signal pattern—three long, two short, one long." Meridia's voice grew softer. "That pattern creates a resonance. A field. The deep-sea crystal amplifies the light into something the human eye cannot see—a barrier that hides our territory from those who would find it."

"From 'the others,'" Sam said.

Meridia nodded. "They call themselves Oceanus Deep Mining. A corporation. Their survey ships have been mapping the seabed for years, searching for mineral deposits. They have sonar. Submersibles. Machines that see through rock." Her dark eyes met Sam's. "If they find the caverns, they will destroy them. They will destroy us."

"And Aunt Eleanor knew this."

"Eleanor understood better than any surface dweller before her." Meridia reached into a fold of her garment and produced something small, wrapped in woven kelp. She held it out. "This was hers. She would want you to have it."

Sam unwrapped it slowly. A crystal rested in the palm—smaller than the ones in the lens, no larger than a thumb, but alive with the same faint blue light. It was warm.

"She carried it always," Meridia said. "It helped her hear the song. Helped her understand. She spent forty years in this tower, lighting the beacon every night, watching over us. She loved the sea, but she loved the world above too. She never stopped being part of both."

"You said she chose to stay below. As an ambassador."

"Yes." Meridia's voice was gentle. "Three years ago, the survey ships came closer than ever before. Eleanor saw that the old compact could not last forever. She went below to help us prepare—to teach us about your world, to learn what we might need if we could no longer hide. She is alive, Samuel. She walks in our cities beneath the sea. She has become a bridge between the tides."

Sam stared at the crystal. Aunt Eleanor. Alive. Not dead, not missing—*chosen*. She had given up the world above, given up her name, her house, her place among humans, to protect a people who had become her own.

And now the lighthouse had been dark for three years. And the survey ships were still coming.

Sam walked to the lantern-room window. The sun was low, bleeding orange across the horizon. The sea stretched out, dark and vast, hiding everything beneath its surface—the grotto, the city, the Tide Callers, Eleanor.

"Three long, two short, one long," Sam said quietly.

"Yes."

Sam looked down at the crystal in the hand. It pulsed faintly, warm against the skin, as though it recognized the weight of the moment.

"I don't know how to work the light," Sam said. "I don't know the signals. I'm not a keeper."

Meridia smiled—that same ancient, alien expression. "Neither was Eleanor, when she first climbed these stairs. The light teaches. The crystal helps. And you carry her blood." She turned toward the stairwell. "I will leave you to decide. But know this: we do not ask lightly. And we do not forget those who answer."

She descended, her footsteps fading into the iron spiral, until the only sound was the wind and the distant cry of gulls.

Sam stood alone in the lantern room, the crystal glowing softly in the hand, watching the sun sink toward the sea.

The choice was not a question of right or wrong. It was a question of whose world Sam belonged to now.
